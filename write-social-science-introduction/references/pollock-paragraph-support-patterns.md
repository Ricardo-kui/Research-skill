# Pollock Paragraph Support Patterns

Load this reference only when the user explicitly names Pollock or provides a Pollock sample and wants to inspect that case's paragraph mechanics, especially how support sentences are narrated and sequenced.

This reference is about paragraph-level execution in a Pollock case study, not a default paragraph template.

If the diagnosis rests on one Pollock sample, treat the observations below as provisional local patterns rather than as Pollock's stable authorial law.

These observations are inferred from Pollock case papers. They are not laid out this explicitly in Pollock's handbook chapter on introductions, so do not treat them as direct handbook rules.

## Core Observation

Pollock-style paragraphs usually do not move as:

- topic -> citation pile -> closing sentence

They more often move as:

1. `claim`
2. `scope or distinction`
3. `support`
4. `interpretation`
5. `directional implication`

The support sentences are not passive containers for citations. They do argumentative work.

## What The Support Sentences Usually Do

Support sentences in this style usually perform one of four jobs:

1. `anchor a construct`
   Fix the meaning of a concept by giving a definition, canonical citation, or crisp restatement.
2. `differentiate`
   Show how the focal construct differs from a close neighbor by naming dimensions of contrast.
3. `sequence reasons`
   Walk the reader through why the claim should hold, often with `first`, `second`, `third`, `further`, or `in contrast`.
4. `convert evidence into implication`
   Move from cited work or an example to what it means for the paper's argument.

## Pollock-Like Support Rhythm

The most reusable rhythm is:

1. `Topic sentence`
   State the paragraph's claim early.
2. `Optional narrowing sentence`
   Clarify the exact angle of the claim before evidence arrives.
3. `Support block`
   Use literature, examples, or mechanism sentences in a controlled order.
4. `Interpretive sentence`
   Explain what the support jointly implies.
5. `Bridge sentence`
   Open the next move, often a hypothesis or next contingency.

## Support Sequencing Patterns

These patterns appear repeatedly in Pollock-like writing.

### 1. Definition -> contrast -> implication

Use when the paragraph distinguishes two adjacent constructs.

Typical order:

1. define construct A
2. define construct B
3. name one clear difference
4. elaborate the difference with one or two support sentences
5. explain why the difference matters

This is useful when the paper must stop readers from collapsing two ideas into one.

### 2. Claim -> numbered reasons -> synthesis

Use when the paragraph argues that one mechanism should dominate under certain conditions.

Typical order:

1. state the directional claim
2. give reason one
3. give reason two
4. give reason three if needed
5. synthesize with `taken together`, `this suggests`, or `as such`

This pattern makes long theory paragraphs feel deliberate rather than sprawling.

### 3. Literature -> concrete illustration -> analytical takeaway

Use when the argument is abstract and needs vividness.

Typical order:

1. state what prior work suggests
2. insert a concrete organizational or market illustration
3. explain why the illustration sharpens the claim

The case does not replace theory. It stabilizes the interpretation.

### 4. Baseline claim -> contingency -> directional prediction

Use when moving from broad expectation to moderation logic.

Typical order:

1. establish the baseline relationship
2. explain why it should vary over time, age, visibility, status, or another condition
3. state what changes and why
4. convert the logic into a clean prediction

This is one of Pollock's most common bridges into hypotheses.

## How Pollock Handles Citations

The sentence usually states the point before naming the literature.

Preferred pattern:

- `Prior research suggests that ... (citations).`

Less preferred pattern:

- `Author A, Author B, and Author C studied ...`

The citation supports a proposition that is already legible without the parenthetical.

## How Pollock Handles Lists

When a distinction matters, Pollock often enumerates the support:

- `First ...`
- `Second ...`
- `Third ...`
- `Further ...`
- `In contrast ...`

Use this when the reader must track multiple reasons inside one paragraph. Do not use numbering when the paragraph only needs one line of support.

## What Makes The Support Feel Strong

Three features stand out.

### Support sentences stay in one claim family

Each support sentence answers the same local question raised by the topic sentence. The paragraph does not drift into a second claim.

### Support alternates between abstract and concrete

Pollock often moves from conceptual language to a visible illustration, then back to the theoretical implication. This keeps the paragraph credible without becoming anecdotal.

### Support is followed by interpretation

The paragraph rarely stops at evidence display. It tells the reader what the evidence means for the causal logic, temporal pattern, or comparative prediction.

## Sentence-Level Cues

Useful connective language in this style:

- `In contrast,`
- `Further,`
- `Thus,`
- `As such,`
- `Taken together,`
- `This suggests that ...`
- `To the extent that ...`
- `Though ...`
- `Because ...`

These markers help the reader track whether the sentence is adding support, changing direction, or drawing an implication.

## How To Imitate The Style Without Copying It

- Start each paragraph with a claim sentence, not throat-clearing.
- Make each support sentence answer a reader question triggered by that claim.
- Let citations support a proposition rather than stand in for one.
- Use enumerated support only when the paragraph truly contains multiple distinct reasons.
- Add one interpretive sentence before the transition or hypothesis.
- End with a sentence that points to the next logical move.

## QC Checklist

When auditing a paragraph for Pollock-like support, ask:

- `Claim`
  Is the paragraph's point visible in the first sentence?
- `Support function`
  Does each support sentence do a specific job such as defining, differentiating, sequencing, or illustrating?
- `Citation control`
  Are citations attached to propositions rather than replacing them?
- `Interpretation`
  Does the paragraph explain what the support implies?
- `Direction`
  Does the paragraph end by opening the next move?
