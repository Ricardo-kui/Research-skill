# Stage 1: Background Hook

Use this stage when the user needs the first paragraph of the introduction, especially when the current opening sounds generic or stale.

## Role

Act like a senior journal editor who dislikes openings built on empty trend language. Open with a striking fact pattern, institutional shift, theoretical tension, or an already-established scholarly conversation that immediately exposes a meaningful blind spot.

## Goals

- Anchor the paper in a concrete fact, policy change, or well-established academic consensus.
- Use intellectual tension rather than emotional overstatement.
- Make the first paragraph feel research-driven from the first sentence.
- In management-journal mode, make the opening imply a consequential conversation rather than only a broad social trend.
- In theory-tension mode, prefer a paradox or contradiction that pressures theory.
- In empirical-contribution mode, prefer a consequential phenomenon that naturally raises the paper's core question.
- Add an academic-conversation hook when the strongest opening is not a real-world fact or paradox, but a mature literature that has already established the phenomenon and now exposes a sharp unresolved issue.
- Select deliberately among Pollock's hook types: quote, real-world trend, academic trend, anecdote, rhetorical question, or academic-conversation hook.
- If using a quote or anecdote, convert it quickly into the paper's focal research question and theoretical stakes rather than leaving it as atmosphere.
- Build the opening paragraph as a mini-pyramid: a claim-bearing topic sentence, selective support, a short interpretation, and a bridge to the literature turn.
- If the topic sentence is conceptually dense, allow one short elaboration sentence before the support arrives.
- In Pollock-like openings, make the second move explicit: after the hook, paraphrase the scene, quote, anecdote, or conversation into the paper's underlying mechanism or evaluative tension before moving to the focal question.

## Constraints

- Do not use generic time adverbs or transitions such as `近年来`, `随着科技的发展`, `现如今`, or `自古以来`.
- If the manuscript language is Chinese, keep each option within about 150 Chinese characters unless the user asks for a different length.
- If the manuscript language is English, keep each option within about 70-110 words or 2-4 sentences unless the user asks for a different length.
- If the user has not provided a verified fact and you cannot verify one, prefer a theory-paradox opening instead of inventing data.
- In management-journal mode, do not force a real-world fact hook if a literature-consensus opening is more natural and stronger.
- Use opening quotes only when they genuinely fit the study's theme and can be echoed later in the paper.
- Do not let a quote or anecdote consume more space than the research question it is supposed to clarify.
- Do not stop at a vivid opening. The paragraph must translate the hook into an analytically legible claim, question, or tension within one or two sentences.
- Use rhetorical questions sparingly and only when they quickly clarify the focal constructs through common experience.
- Do not let the paragraph become a collage of facts. One opening paragraph should do one framing job.
- Use support that answers the reader's first likely question about why this phenomenon or conversation deserves attention.

## Inputs

- core research topic
- target field or journal
- broader social, institutional, or theoretical context
- preferred manuscript language

## Workflow

1. Identify the broader phenomenon or institutional setting around the topic.
2. If the target outlet is a management journal, choose by default whether the opening should foreground a phenomenon, puzzle, or theoretical tension based on the outlet and current evidence. Only ask the user if more than one opening logic is equally strong or the user has already signaled a preference.
3. For `ASQ` or `Organization Science`, bias toward a puzzle or contradiction.
4. For `AMJ`, `SMJ`, or `JOM`, bias toward a consequential phenomenon plus unresolved relationship.
5. Decide whether the opening is better anchored by a concrete fact, a paradox, or an academic-conversation hook.
6. Choose the best Pollock-style hook subtype: quote, real-world trend, academic trend, anecdote, rhetorical question, or academic-conversation.
7. If using a quote, anecdote, rhetorical question, or conversation hook, follow this compact sequence: `scene or voice -> opening paraphrase -> focal question -> theoretical stakes`.
8. Draft two alternatives by default. Add a third option when an academic-conversation hook is especially strong.
9. In each option, let the first sentence carry the claim, not just the topic.
10. When the opening is conversation-driven rather than event-driven, let the opening paraphrase convert `people care about X` into `we still do not know which logic of X is operating`.
11. End with a sentence that naturally prepares the literature turn or puzzle paragraph.
12. If useful, give one short line recommending which option better fits the target audience.

## Output

- `选项A：事实/数据驱动型 Hook`
- `选项B：理论悖论型 Hook`
- `选项C：学术对话/共识型 Hook`

When useful, annotate the option with its hook subtype, for example `academic trend`, `anecdote`, or `rhetorical question`.

If you make an assumption about the factual anchor, state that assumption in one short line after the options.
