# Pollock Introduction Framework

Load this reference when drafting, revising, or auditing a management-journal introduction with an explicit Pollock overlay.

This reference is the primary Pollock overlay for introductions. It compresses the guidance Pollock gives in *How to Use Storytelling in Your Academic Writing* on book pages 52-66, together with the Beorchia introduction checklist that Pollock endorses.

Use this reference before `pollock-case-patterns.md`. The handbook tells you what Pollock explicitly recommends; the case-patterns file shows how those recommendations appear across several Pollock papers.
Use `pollock-beorchia-checklist.md` when you need to turn these principles into a drafting or auditing worksheet.

## Why The Introduction Matters

- The title gets readers to notice the paper.
- The abstract gets them through the door.
- The introduction sets the hook and determines whether readers, reviewers, and editors keep reading with interest or with suspicion.
- In review settings, the introduction is the interpretive frame for the rest of the paper.
- Pollock treats the introduction as the most critical section for publication success, even though it is only a small share of the article.

## Scope And Length

- Treat the introduction as the first few pages before the theory and hypotheses section.
- Do not define the whole front end before methods as the introduction.
- Default to about three to four double-spaced pages for a management-journal article unless the journal or manuscript type clearly calls for something else.

## Three Core Questions

An effective management-journal introduction should answer these questions in order:

1. `Who cares?`
2. `What do we know, what don't we know, and so what?`
3. `What will we learn?`

If one of these questions is missing, the front end is probably not ready.

## Hook Menu

Use one of these hook types:

- `quote`
  Use only when it tightly captures the paper's theme and can be referenced later in the paper.
- `real-world trend`
  Use when change in practice, industry, or society makes the question urgent or visible.
- `academic trend`
  Use when a mature literature has paid serious attention to the topic but not resolved it.
- `anecdote`
  Use when a short narrative example gives the paper a human face or sharpens the paradox.
- `rhetorical question`
  Use when it prompts readers to draw on common experience and quickly understand the focal constructs.
- `academic-conversation hook`
  Use when the strongest opening is a well-established scholarly conversation that already implies a consequential unresolved issue.

Default rules:

- For `ASQ` and `Organization Science`, prefer `academic trend`, `academic-conversation hook`, or a paradox-rich anecdote.
- For `AMJ`, `SMJ`, and `JOM`, prefer `real-world trend`, `academic trend`, or a bounded rhetorical question tied to the paper's core question.
- Use the quote hook only when it clearly fits the paper's theme and can be referenced later rather than serving as decorative theater.

## Opening Paraphrase Discipline

After the hook, Pollock-like introductions usually do not linger in scene-setting mode. They paraphrase the hook into the paper's underlying mechanism, evaluative tension, or focal research question.

Useful sequences:

- `quote or anecdote -> paraphrase the underlying social or cognitive mechanism -> focal question`
- `phenomenon -> explain why the phenomenon is not straightforward -> focal question`
- `academic conversation -> paraphrase what the current conversation implies -> focal insufficiency`

If the opening still reads like atmosphere after the second or third sentence, the hook has not yet been converted into argument.

## Establish The Conversation

Choose one mode for establishing the conversation:

- `synthesized coherence`
  Bridge two or more conversations that share an underlying concern but have not been linked explicitly.
- `progressive coherence`
  Show how a single conversation has developed and why it now needs to move forward.
- `noncoherence`
  Highlight conflicting findings, opposing perspectives, or unresolved debate and show how the study adjudicates among them.

Do not warehouse citations. Use literature to make the conversation visible.

Locke and Golden-Biddle's three approaches are especially useful here:

- `synthesized coherence`
- `progressive coherence`
- `noncoherence`

## Problematize The Conversation

After establishing the conversation, choose the right level of problematization:

- `incompleteness`
  There is more to know; the literature has overlooked something important.
- `inadequacy`
  The literature's current framing is insufficient because it misses a relevant perspective, mechanism, or explanation.
- `incommensurability`
  The dominant view is wrong, misleading, or not applicable to the focal setting.

Prefer the language of `puzzle`, `paradox`, `controversy`, or `unresolved relationship` over weak `gap` language.

Three useful problematization levels are:

- `incompleteness`
- `inadequacy`
- `incommensurability`

Choose the weakest level that your evidence can defend.

## State Why The Omission Matters

Pollock-style introductions do not stop after identifying what the literature misses. They usually add one sentence explaining why the omission matters.

Typical functions:

- show that current theory would overgeneralize without the missing mechanism
- show that practical evaluation or interpretation would be distorted
- show that the current empirical relationship cannot yet be interpreted cleanly

Treat this as part of `What do we know, what don't we know, and so what?`, not as an optional flourish.

## Explain What Readers Will Learn

The contribution paragraph should not merely say what the paper studies. It should show how the study changes the conversation.

Two useful contribution logics:

- `consensus shifting`
  Challenge or bound a widely held assumption.
- `consensus creation`
  Clarify or reconcile discrepant lines of thought.

When possible, explain why the paper is interesting by showing what prior belief shifts, what conflict gets clarified, or what hidden pattern becomes visible.

If the reader still cannot say what prior belief should move after reading the introduction, the contribution is under-explained.

In empirical Pollock-like papers, a strong contribution paragraph often answers four questions in one tight sequence:

1. `What does the paper examine?`
2. `Why can this setting or design answer the question?`
3. `What does the paper show or argue?`
4. `What conversation should move because of that answer?`

## Common Pitfalls

- Failing to motivate and problematize the study
- Assuming the value of the study is obvious
- Treating being `the first` as a contribution by itself
- Filling a minor gap instead of addressing a meaningful puzzle
- Cramming too much into the introduction so it becomes long and rambling
- Using rhetorical fireworks without saying clearly what the paper is about
- Spending more than a sentence or so describing the paper's structure
- Overpromising beyond what the evidence can support

## Checklist For Drafting Or QC

Use this checklist when drafting or auditing:

- `Hook`
  Does the opening capture attention with a fitting hook type?
- `Conversation`
  Is the relevant theoretical conversation clearly identified?
- `Problematization`
  Is the puzzle, paradox, or controversy explicit and important?
- `Contribution`
  Does the introduction explain what readers will learn?
- `Theory and practice`
  Is the paper's relevance visible where appropriate?
- `Focus`
  Is the introduction tight rather than sprawling?
- `Promise discipline`
  Do the claims match what the paper can actually deliver?
- `Roadmap discipline`
  Is the structure paragraph absent, short, or clearly justified?
