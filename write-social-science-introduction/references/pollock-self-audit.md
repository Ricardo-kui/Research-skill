# Pollock Self Audit

Load this reference when the user wants a compact Chinese self-check sheet, a final pre-submission audit, or a fast yes/no review of an introduction under a Pollock overlay.

This reference is a concise audit tool derived from:

- `pollock-introduction-framework.md`
- `pollock-beorchia-checklist.md`
- `pollock-alignment-matrix.md`

Use it after drafting or when comparing a draft against Pollock-style expectations.

## Core Questions

Check whether the introduction clearly answers:

1. `Who cares?`
2. `What do we know / not know / so what?`
3. `What will we learn?`

If any one of these is fuzzy, the front end is still weak.

## Pollock Self-Check List

### 1. Hook

- Does the introduction grab attention in the first paragraph?
- Is the hook type well chosen?
  - quote
  - trend
  - anecdote
  - rhetorical question
- If using a quote, does it truly fit the theme and get referenced later?

### 2. Topic and Question

- Is the research topic or research question clearly stated?
- Can a reader tell by the first two paragraphs what the paper is about?

### 3. Importance

- Is theoretical importance visible?
- Is practical importance visible where relevant?
- Is at least one form of importance concrete rather than generic?

### 4. Existing Knowledge

- Does the draft state what we already know?
- Does it distinguish key theoretical findings from key empirical findings when helpful?
- Does it establish a conversation rather than merely list citations?

### 5. Conversation Structure

Which conversation structure is being used?

- synthesized coherence
- progressive coherence
- noncoherence

Is the choice clear and well executed?

### 6. Puzzle

- Is the unaddressed issue framed as a puzzle, paradox, controversy, or unresolved relationship?
- Does the draft avoid weak `gap` language?

### 7. Problematization

Which level is being used?

- incompleteness
- inadequacy
- incommensurability

- Is this level justified by the evidence?
- Does the introduction explain why the omission matters?

### 8. What Will We Learn?

- Does the introduction clearly state what readers will learn?
- Can the reader say what prior belief or debate will move?

### 9. Contribution Logic

Is the contribution framed as:

- consensus shifting
- consensus creation

Or is it merely a new setting, sample, or method?

### 10. Paper Move

- Does the introduction include a clean paper-move sentence once the gap is sharp enough?

Typical forms:

- `In this study ...`
- `The present study ...`
- `In this paper we examine ...`

### 11. Setting or Design Justification

For empirical papers:

- Is the setting or design justified as part of the argument?
- Does the draft explain why this context can answer the focal question?

### 12. Roadmap Discipline

- Is the structure description short?
- Could the roadmap be cut to one sentence or removed?

### 13. Focus

- Is the introduction focused rather than long and rambling?
- Does each paragraph do one main job?

### 14. Common Pitfalls

Check for these red flags:

- assumes the topic is obviously important
- treats being first as the contribution
- focuses on gap filling instead of a real problem
- uses rhetorical fireworks without saying what the paper is about
- spends too much time on structure
- overpromises relative to the evidence
- lets the claimed contribution drift away from the rest of the paper

### 15. Three-Paragraph Test

By the end of the first three paragraphs, can the reader answer:

1. What is this paper about?
2. What does the literature already say?
3. What exactly is missing or unresolved?
4. What does this paper do?
5. Why should this change the conversation?

If not, revise before polishing prose.
