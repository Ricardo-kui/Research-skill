# Pollock Alignment Matrix

Load this reference when the user wants a side-by-side comparison of Pollock's introduction advice, the Beorchia checklist, and Pollock case papers.

This file is meant to help the skill produce comparison tables, audit sheets, or teaching summaries without collapsing three different evidence levels into one.

## Source Roles

| Source | Role | Best use |
|---|---|---|
| `pollock-introduction-framework.md` | principle layer | define what Pollock explicitly recommends |
| `pollock-beorchia-checklist.md` | operational layer | turn the principles into drafting and QC questions |
| `pollock-case-patterns.md` | case layer | show how Pollock's papers actually implement or vary those principles |
| `pollock-paragraph-support-patterns.md` | sentence-level inference layer | infer support sequencing from sample papers without overstating that it is explicit handbook doctrine |

## Introduction Comparison Table

| Dimension | Pollock handbook principle | Beorchia checklist prompt | Pollock case-paper pattern | How to use in the skill |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Overall purpose | The introduction is the most critical section because it sets the hook and interpretive frame. | Check whether the intro captures attention, identifies the conversation, explains the contribution, and shows how the paper will do so. | Pollock papers usually make the front end do visible argumentative work early. | Use as the first QC lens before sentence-level diagnosis. |
| Scope and length | Treat the introduction as the pages before theory/hypotheses; usually about three to four double-spaced pages. | Implicit in the outline structure. | Empirical papers often move into theory quickly once the front-end jobs are done. | Use to prevent bloated intros and excessive roadmap text. |
| Three core questions | `Who cares?` -> `What do we know / not know / so what?` -> `What will we learn?` | The checklist is directly organized around these questions. | All strong Pollock cases answer these in some form, though not always with explicit labels. | Use as the main comparison frame when evaluating a draft. |
| Hook choice | Use quote, trend, anecdote, or rhetorical question depending on fit. | Ask whether the intro captures attention and what hook type is being used. | Pollock cases use different hooks; no single hook is mandatory. | Treat hook as a menu, not a signature move. |
| Quote hook discipline | Use quotes only when they fit the theme and can be referenced later. | Captured under `Who cares?` and hook choice. | The theory article on CEO celebrity uses a quote effectively; not all Pollock papers do. | Do not encourage decorative epigraphs. |
| Conversation building | Establish the theoretical conversation explicitly. | Ask what we know and identify key theoretical and empirical findings. | Pollock papers usually identify a bounded conversation rather than a broad topic area. | Require clear conversation naming before contribution claims. |
| Conversation structure | Use synthesized coherence, progressive coherence, or noncoherence. | The checklist explicitly asks the writer to choose one. | Different Pollock papers use different structures depending on the problem. | Good for classifying the literature turn in a comparison table. |
| Problematization | Prefer puzzle, paradox, controversy, or unresolved relationship over generic gap language. | Ask for the unaddressed puzzle, controversy, or paradox. | Pollock papers often problematize by inadequacy rather than mild gap filling. | Use this to sharpen the gap paragraph or diagnose weak motivation. |
| Problematization strength | Choose among incompleteness, inadequacy, and incommensurability. | The checklist explicitly asks the writer to choose one. | Most Pollock empirical papers look like inadequacy; stronger theory essays sometimes approach incommensurability. | Choose the weakest defensible level. |
| Importance of the omission | Identifying the puzzle is not enough; explain why it matters. | The checklist asks `Why does this need to be addressed?` | Pollock papers usually spend at least one sentence converting the omission into consequences for theory or evaluation. | Use to prevent empty gap statements. |
| Contribution logic | Explain what readers will learn; often via consensus shifting or consensus creation. | The checklist explicitly asks for these two options. | Pollock cases often preview what prior belief moves, especially in contribution paragraphs. | Use to force a real `so what` statement. |
| Theory and practice | Pollock values both, especially for the `Who cares?` section and contribution. | The checklist separates theory and practice under both importance and contribution. | Pollock cases vary in how explicit practice relevance is, but the possibility is always available. | Keep practice relevance visible when it helps broaden interest. |
| Paper move sentence | State what the paper does once the gap is sharp enough. | Captured under `Describe current study`. | `In this study ...`, `The present study ...`, and `In this paper we examine ...` recur across cases. | Encourage an early, clean paper-move sentence. |
| Setting or design justification | Not foregrounded as a separate doctrine in the handbook, but consistent with explaining how the paper will accomplish its goals. | The checklist asks the writer to articulate how the paper will accomplish its goals. | Pollock empirical papers often justify the setting as part of the argument. | Treat as a common case implementation, not a universal Pollock law. |
| Roadmap discipline | Do not spend much time on structure. | The checklist flags spending too much time on structure as a mistake. | Some Pollock papers use a short handoff; some have almost none. | Limit the roadmap to about a sentence unless there is a clear need. |
| Common pitfalls | Do not assume importance is obvious, do not rely on being first, do not gap-fill trivially, do not overpromise, do not ramble. | The checklist turns these into concrete yes/no audit items. | The case papers generally avoid these pitfalls by making the puzzle and move visible. | Use as the final red-flag screen before closing a draft. |
| Paragraph openings | Not a main handbook doctrine, but consistent with direct, reader-oriented exposition. | Not explicit in the checklist. | Pollock case paragraphs often open with claim-first sentences. | Treat as a robust case tendency, not an explicit Pollock rule. |
| Support-sentence sequencing | Not explicitly formalized in the handbook chapter. | Not explicit in the checklist. | The case papers often use claim -> support -> implication or claim -> reasons -> synthesis. | Use only in paragraph-analysis mode and label as case inference. |

## What The Matrix Clarifies

- The handbook gives the strongest evidence about introduction-level goals and choices.
- The checklist gives the cleanest drafting and self-audit prompts.
- The case papers show repeated habits, but not every repeated habit is a prescribed rule.
- Sentence-level support sequencing belongs to the weakest evidence tier and should be labeled accordingly.

## Recommended Comparison Output

When the user asks for a Pollock comparison table, organize the answer in this order:

1. `principles`
2. `operational checklist`
3. `case implementation`
4. `stable versus optional`

This order prevents case details from overruling the handbook.
