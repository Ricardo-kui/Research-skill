# Pollock Case Patterns

Load this reference when the user provides multiple Pollock papers and wants a stronger case-based synthesis.

This reference summarizes patterns observed across these Pollock cases:

1. `Now You See Me: How Status and Categorical Proximity Shape Misconduct Scandalization`
2. `A Tale of Two Assets: The Effects of Firm Reputation and Celebrity on Earnings Surprises and Investors' Reactions`
3. `Believing One's Own Press: The Causes and Consequences of CEO Celebrity`
4. `Are More Resources Always Better for Growth? Resource Stickiness in Market and Product Expansion`

Supplementary validation cases that sharpen the patterns below:

5. `Overpaid CEOs and Underpaid Managers: Fairness and Executive Compensation`
6. `The Burden of Celebrity: the Impact of CEO Certification Contests on CEO Pay and Performance`

These cases support stronger claims than a single-sample diagnosis, but they still do not justify treating every observed move as a universal Pollock law.

Use this reference after `pollock-introduction-framework.md`. The handbook gives the explicit method; this file shows how that method appears, bends, or varies across actual Pollock papers.

## Evidence Boundary

- `4 core papers plus 2 supplementary cases`
  Strong enough to identify recurring preferences and a more modern compact AMJ variant.
- `not exhaustive`
  Treat the patterns below as well-supported tendencies, not immutable authorial rules.

## Cross-Case Introduction Layout

Two broad layouts recur.

### 1. Empirical Pollock layout

Common in the misconduct, intangible-assets, and growth papers:

1. `phenomenon or conversation opening`
2. `precise inadequacy in current work`
3. `paper move`
4. `study preview or setting justification`
5. `results or contribution preview`
6. `brief structure handoff or direct transition into theory`

### 2. Theory-article Pollock layout

Observed in the CEO celebrity paper:

1. `vivid quote or anecdotal opening`
2. `abstract mechanism behind the anecdote`
3. `paper's central question and model`
4. `second-order question or consequence`
5. `short roadmap`

Do not force the theory-article layout onto empirical papers.

### 3. Compact AMJ-style variant

Visible in newer Pollock-linked empirical work such as the scandalization paper:

1. `state a known but consequential relationship`
2. `show the missing mechanism or media process`
3. `name the paper move`
4. `justify the setting and data as diagnostic`
5. `preview the bounded answer and contribution`

This variant is tighter than the older theory article and less theatrical than a quote-led opening.

## Handbook Alignment

The following cross-case patterns are directly supported by Pollock's handbook guidance:

- introductions should answer `Who cares?`, `What do we know / not know / so what?`, and `What will we learn?`
- hooks can be quotes, trends, anecdotes, or rhetorical questions
- the conversation should be established and then problematized
- the contribution should clarify what readers will learn
- structure description should be brief
- overpromising is a serious risk

The case papers also suggest some tendencies that the handbook does not prescribe as rigid rules, such as:

- early explicit paper-move sentences
- integrated setting justification
- highly visible contribution sentences near the end of the introduction
- a disciplined opening paraphrase after vivid hooks or conversation openings
- a gap-value sentence that explains why the omission distorts theory or evaluation

## Stable Cross-Case Tendencies

### Claim-first paragraphs

Pollock-case paragraphs usually open with a judgment, problem, or claim, not with neutral topic announcement.

Examples of recurring moves:

- `Much of this research, however, has focused ...`
- `The present study attempts to address these gaps ...`
- `In this paper we examine ...`
- `We contribute ...`

### Problematization by inadequacy

These papers rarely rely on weak `the literature is limited` language. They more often say:

- existing work overlooks a specific mechanism
- current concepts are blurred or fragmented
- current treatments decontextualize the focal relationship
- prior definitions or strategy labels are too coarse for the focal question

### Paper move before long exposition

Once the gap is sharp enough, Pollock tends to state what the paper does relatively early with a sentence such as:

- `In this study ...`
- `The present study ...`
- `In this paper we examine ...`

### Setting or design justification as argument

In the empirical papers, the setting is not just reported. It is justified as part of the paper's logic.

Typical function:

- show why the setting reveals the mechanism
- show why the focal outcome is theoretically meaningful
- show why the data can distinguish among explanations

### Explicit contribution language

At least some Pollock papers make the contribution very visible near the end of the introduction rather than leaving it implicit.

## Recurring Narrative Moves

### 1. Importance -> complication

Open on an important phenomenon or valued outcome, then quickly introduce the countervailing complication.

Example pattern:

- growth is desirable
- growth also creates complexity
- therefore the strategic dilemma matters

### 2. Interest -> fragmentation

Open by noting strong scholarly attention, then pivot to conceptual fragmentation or construct confusion.

Example pattern:

- scholars care about social approval assets
- labels and proxies have proliferated
- therefore the field lacks coherent theory

### 3. Accepted relationship -> missing mechanism

Open on a relationship that is already broadly accepted, then show that the process producing it remains unclear.

Example pattern:

- high status increases scandalization
- but we do not know why some misconduct gets publicized
- therefore the media process needs explanation

### 4. Concrete anecdote -> abstract mechanism

More common in the theory article:

- start with a vivid quote
- explain the social-cognitive pattern behind it
- turn the anecdote into a general mechanism

### 5. Hook -> opening paraphrase -> focal question

Common in the quote-led and rhetorical-question openings:

- start with a vivid voice, scene, or socially recognizable contrast
- paraphrase the scene into the paper's underlying mechanism or evaluative tension
- convert that tension into one focal question, and sometimes a second consequence question

This is the most important front-end move that weak imitations often miss. Without the paraphrase step, the opening feels stylish but unanalytical.

### 6. Conversation -> inadequacy -> value of resolving it

Common in the reputation, celebrity, and scandalization papers:

- establish what the field already believes
- show why the current framing is too coarse, fragmented, or mechanism-thin
- explain what remains misread if the issue is not resolved

This makes the gap feel consequential rather than merely unstudied.

## Paragraph-Level Logic

Across the cases, paragraphs often move as:

1. `topic sentence with evaluative claim`
2. `support that stays in one claim family`
3. `interpretive sentence`
4. `bridge to the next issue`

### Common topic-sentence types

- `problem sentence`
  `Much of this research ...`
- `paper-move sentence`
  `In this study ...`
- `mechanism sentence`
  `As status perceptions are inherently relational ...`
- `contrast sentence`
  `This is problematic because ...`

### Common support-sentence functions

- build a consensus point from literature
- sharpen why the existing treatment is insufficient
- define or contrast constructs
- use one concrete illustration to stabilize an abstract claim
- justify why a setting or outcome is theoretically diagnostic

### Common support sequencing

- `claim -> literature support -> why this is problematic`
- `claim -> first / second reasons -> synthesis`
- `claim -> example -> generalized implication`
- `paper move -> design/setting rationale -> why the design matters`

## Topic-Sentence and Support-Sentence Craft

### Topic sentences

The first sentence usually does argumentative work immediately. It tells the reader what the paragraph is doing.

### Support sentences

Support sentences tend to do one of three things:

1. `substantiate`
   Show the claim is grounded in literature or visible facts.
2. `differentiate`
   Explain how one construct, condition, or logic differs from another.
3. `activate implication`
   Turn the evidence into a reason the next paragraph must happen.

## How Gap Value Is Usually Argued

Across the empirical cases, the gap-value sentence often does one of three things:

1. `theory calibration`
   It shows that the current literature would misstate when, why, or for whom a known relationship holds.
2. `evaluation correction`
   It shows that observers, audiences, or decision makers would interpret outcomes incorrectly without the missing distinction.
3. `mechanism clarification`
   It shows that an established effect still cannot be explained because the process linking cause and outcome remains underspecified.

If a draft identifies the omission but does not perform one of these jobs, it is usually weaker than the Pollock cases.

Citations usually support a proposition that is already legible.

## What Seems Robust Versus Sample-Specific

### Robust across these four cases

- claim-first paragraph openings
- precise inadequacy framing
- early paper-move sentences
- support sentences that do argumentative work
- integrated setting or mechanism justification

### More sample-specific

- opening with a long quote
- explicit multi-question setup in the introduction
- highly visible contribution paragraph at the end of the intro
- a formal roadmap paragraph before theory

These should not be treated as mandatory Pollock signatures.
