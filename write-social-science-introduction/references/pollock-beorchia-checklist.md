# Pollock Beorchia Checklist

Load this reference when the user wants a Pollock-style self-audit, outline scaffold, or comparison table for an introduction.

This reference is a structured companion to `pollock-introduction-framework.md`. It is derived from the Beorchia outline/checklist recommended by Grant and Pollock for constructing an AMJ-worthy introduction.

Use it as an operational worksheet, not as a replacement for judgment.

## Overarching Goals

Check whether the introduction does all four:

1. capture the reader's attention
2. identify the conversation
3. explain the paper's contribution
4. articulate how the paper will accomplish those goals

## Section 1: Establish Importance

Answer:

- `Who cares / so what?`
- `What is the research topic or question?`
- `Why is this research interesting and important in theory?`
- `Why is this research interesting and important in practice?`

Typical hook menu:

- quote
- trend
- anecdote
- rhetorical question

## Section 2: Highlight Past Research

Answer:

- `What do we already know about the topic or question?`
- `What are the key theoretical findings?`
- `What are the key empirical findings?`

Then identify the unaddressed puzzle, controversy, or paradox using one of these conversation structures:

- `synthesized coherence`
  Bridge two conversations that should be talking to each other.
- `progressive coherence`
  Show how one conversation needs to move forward.
- `noncoherence`
  Present competing perspectives and explain how the paper will resolve them.

## Section 3: Problematize The Study

Choose the best-fitting level:

- `incompleteness`
  There is more to know.
- `inadequacy`
  Existing work misses important perspectives or mechanisms.
- `incommensurability`
  Existing framing is inaccurate or misleading.

Then answer:

- `Why does this omission or problem matter?`

Do not stop at naming the puzzle. Explain why it deserves attention.

## Section 4: Describe The Current Study

Answer:

- `What will we learn?`
- `How does the study change, challenge, or advance understanding?`

Two common contribution logics:

- `consensus shifting`
  Challenge or bound widely held assumptions.
- `consensus creation`
  Clarify the lines of debate or resolve a conflict.

Then make the contributions explicit:

- `theory`
- `practice`

## Common Mistakes

### Fail to motivate and problematize

- assume the value is obvious
- assume there is inherent value in being the first
- focus on gap filling instead of a question, problem, puzzle, or paradox

### Lack of focus

- cram too much into the introduction
- use rhetorical fireworks and never state what the paper is about
- spend too much time describing paper structure

### Overpromising

- set expectations the paper cannot deliver on
- let the introduction's research question and contributions drift away from the rest of the paper
- make claims so extravagant they feel self-serving
