# One-Page Research Preflight Checklist

## 1. Research Question

- Is the question genuinely important, rather than merely topical?
- Does it address a real theoretical tension, substantive phenomenon, or identification opportunity?
- Can I state the research question in one or two sharp sentences?
- If the project succeeds, who will care and why?

## 2. Positioning

- Have I clearly identified the 3-5 closest papers?
- Is it obvious whether my paper offers direct evidence, adjacent evidence, or a mechanism bridge?
- Have I stated exactly how my paper differs from the closest literature?
- Does the paper change an existing conversation, or merely extend it at the margin?

## 3. Contribution

- Can I describe the contribution without relying on the word “gap”?
- Does the contribution appear early, or does the reader have to infer it?
- Am I offering a new explanation, a new boundary condition, a new phenomenon, or a cleaner design?
- After reading the paper, what should the reader understand differently?

## 4. Theory and Mechanism

- Does the theory explain why `X` affects `Y`, rather than merely naming concepts?
- Is the mechanism chain internally coherent?
- If multiple theories are used, are they integrated into one line of argument?
- Do the focal constructs map cleanly onto the empirical measures?

## 5. Design and Evidence

- Is the paper making a causal claim or documenting a strong association? Is that explicit?
- What is the most vulnerable step in the empirical design?
- Are the core measures valid, interpretable, and sufficiently close to the constructs?
- Do the findings speak directly to the stated research question?

## 6. Front-End Writing

- After reading the introduction, does the reader know the question, motivation, design, findings, and contribution?
- Is the introduction concise enough to preserve the paper’s “golden real estate”?
- Does the literature discussion serve the argument, rather than interrupt it?
- Is the front end organized by logic rather than by accumulation?

## 7. Desk-Reject Risk

- Would an editor see this as a meaningful contribution rather than a narrow extension?
- Is any claim stronger than the evidence can support?
- Is there any overcitation, especially in the introduction?
- If a skeptical reviewer wanted to reject the paper quickly, what would they attack first?

## 8. Submission Readiness

- Is the paper’s main contribution visible on page one?
- Are theory, design, and evidence aligned?
- Is the managerial or substantive significance clear?
- If the paper were rejected today, would the reason be the question, the design, the writing, or the framing?

## Current Agenda Reminders

- For `product recall`, emphasize high-consequence timing decisions rather than generic product safety.
- For `organizational learning`, emphasize how failure signals are interpreted and translated into action.
- For `policy shocks`, emphasize how institutions shape strategic response rather than merely documenting legal change.
